Madagascar carries approximately 1.5% of the global malaria burden. The primary driver is Anopheles mosquito breeding in irrigated rice fields — which flood seasonally from November through April, coinciding precisely with peak malaria transmission.

See the Funding section below.

Closed-loop feed system

Imported fishmeal represents 60–80% of African aquaculture operating costs. This system cuts that to under 30% through three on-site feed sources:

  • Duckweed (Lemna minor) — 25% of diet, zero cost. Grown on fish wastewater. Doubles biomass every 2–3 days. Locally sourceable from nearby ponds.
  • Black Soldier Fly Larvae (BSFL) — 35–50% of diet, near-zero cost. Fed on vegetable scraps and kitchen waste. 15–18 day colony cycle. BSFL frass doubles as organic fertiliser for rice fields.
  • Vegetable trim — supplemental, zero cost. Harvest waste fed directly to fish. System waste approaches zero.
04 — Ecological Safety
Sterile fish.
Contained system.
Independent review.

The project uses Mozambique tilapia (Oreochromis mossambicus) — a species already present in Madagascar for decades. This is not a new introduction. Every fish deployed into rice fields is genetically sterile, certified by flow cytometry.

RiskLevelMitigation
Competition with native speciesModerateTriploid fish cannot reproduce. Quarterly monitoring of natural waterways.
Habitat alterationLow–ModerateRice fields already disturbed by farming. Deployment limited to designated fields.
Escape to natural waterwaysLowFine-mesh tank screens · flood barrier design · triploid fish cannot establish feral populations
Hybridisation riskVery LowAll field fish are sterile triploids — genetic mixing is biologically impossible

Primary mitigation — triploid (sterile) fish

All fish deployed into rice fields are genetically sterile — certified by flow cytometry on each production batch. Sterile fish cannot reproduce, eliminating any risk of feral population establishment. The diploid breeding colony never leaves the secure tank environment. Triploidisation is the same technique now deployed in national-level invasive fish control programmes in Thailand and elsewhere.
